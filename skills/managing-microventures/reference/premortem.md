# Pre-mortem — prospective hindsight

Source: **Gary Klein, "Performing a Project Premortem", *Harvard Business Review* (2007)**, built on
research into **prospective hindsight** by **Mitchell, Russo & Pennington (1989)**, who found that
imagining an outcome as already certain ("this *has* happened") generates roughly **30% more reasons**
for it than asking whether it *might* happen.

## The technique

Before committing, imagine you're in the future and the project has **already failed**:

1. **Set the scene:** "It is 6 months from now. This venture failed — badly. We're certain it's
   dead."
2. **Generate reasons:** everyone independently writes down *why* it failed. The certainty framing
   unlocks doubts people would otherwise suppress.
3. **Collect and rank** the failure reasons by likelihood and impact.
4. **Act:** strengthen the plan against the top reasons, and turn the most dangerous ones into
   concrete tripwires.

## Why "independently, no authority"

The value comes from surfacing **suppressed** concerns. If the boss (or the loudest optimist) speaks
first, people anchor and self-censor. So: generate reasons **independently and silently first**, with
**no authority figure framing the answer** — then pool them. (This is the same anti-groupthink rule
the consilium runs on: judge alone first, synthesize second.)

## Converting reasons into triggers

Each top failure reason becomes a **state+date kill trigger** (see `kill-criteria.md`): not "users
might not retain" but "if day-7 retention is below 15% by 2026-07-01, that reason has come true."

## When to run

- **Gate 1**, as the primary tool for writing the kill line.
- Any time the plan changes materially (a committed pivot → re-run a quick pre-mortem on the new bet).

## How the skill / lenses use it
